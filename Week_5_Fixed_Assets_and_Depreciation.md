## 1. DEFINITION AND RECOGNITION OF FIXED ASSETS

### 1.1 Definition of Fixed Assets (Non-Current Assets)

**Non-current assets (fixed assets)** are assets acquired for long-term use in the business (more than one accounting period). They are not intended for resale in the ordinary course of business (International Accounting Standards Board [IASB], 2003).

Under **IAS 16 — Property, Plant and Equipment**, a fixed asset is defined as a tangible item that:
- Is held for use in the production or supply of goods or services, for rental to others, or for administrative purposes; **and**
- Is expected to be used during more than one accounting period (IASB, 2003, para. 6).

**Examples in Kenya:**
- Land and buildings (e.g., a warehouse in Industrial Area, Nairobi)
- Motor vehicles (e.g., a delivery lorry, matatu, boda boda)
- Plant and machinery (e.g., milling machine at a Thika factory)
- Furniture and fittings (e.g., office desks, display shelves)
- Computer equipment (e.g., POS system at a supermarket)

### 1.2 Recognition Criteria (IAS 16)

A fixed asset is **recognised** (recorded in the books) only when **both** of the following conditions are met (IASB, 2003, para. 7):

1. It is **probable** that future economic benefits associated with the asset will flow to the entity; **and**
2. The **cost** of the asset can be **measured reliably**.

If either condition is not met, the expenditure is treated as an expense in the period it is incurred rather than capitalised as an asset.

**Note on Land:** Land is **not depreciated** because it has an unlimited useful life (except in special cases such as quarries or leasehold land).

## 2. ACQUISITION OF FIXED ASSETS

### 2.1 Definition of Acquisition of Fixed Assets

The **acquisition of a fixed asset** refers to the process by which an entity obtains ownership or control of a non-current asset, whether through outright purchase, construction, exchange, or finance lease, and initially records it in the accounting records at its cost (Weygandt, Kimmel, & Kieso, 2018). The acquisition date is the date on which the acquirer obtains control of the asset and the asset is ready for its intended use.

### 2.2 Historical Cost Principle

Under IAS 16, a fixed asset is **initially measured at cost** — the historical cost principle. The cost comprises (IASB, 2003, para. 16):

- The **purchase price**, including import duties and non-refundable purchase taxes, after deducting trade discounts and rebates
- Any **directly attributable costs** of bringing the asset to the location and condition necessary for it to be capable of operating in the manner intended by management
- The initial estimate of **dismantling and restoration costs**, where applicable

| Asset | Costs Included |
|-------|---------------|
| **Land** | Purchase price, legal fees, stamp duty, survey fees, land rates arrears |
| **Building** | Purchase price or construction cost, architect fees, building permit fees |
| **Motor Vehicle** | Purchase price, delivery charges, registration fees, insurance (first year may be capitalised), modifications for business use |
| **Machinery** | Purchase price, transport/delivery cost, installation cost, testing cost before use |

**Costs that are NOT capitalised** (expensed immediately):
- Routine repairs and maintenance
- Administration and general overhead costs
- Costs incurred after the asset is in its intended working condition

**Example:** Mwangi buys a machine for his factory in Athi River:

| Item | Amount (KES) |
|------|-------------|
| Purchase price | 800,000 |
| Delivery charges | 25,000 |
| Installation costs | 40,000 |
| Testing and trial runs | 15,000 |
| **Total cost to capitalise** | **880,000** |

### 2.3 Recording Acquisitions — Journal Entries

When a fixed asset is acquired, the asset account is **debited** at historical cost and the corresponding credit reflects the method of payment:

| Method of Purchase | Debit | Credit |
|-------------------|-------|--------|
| Cash purchase | Fixed Asset Account | Cash/Bank |
| Credit purchase | Fixed Asset Account | Supplier (Creditor) |
| Loan-financed | Fixed Asset Account | Loan Account |

**Worked Example:**

Wanjiku Transport, Nairobi, purchases a delivery van for KES 2,000,000 on credit from Nairobi Motors Ltd.

**Journal Entry:**

| Date | Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 Jan 2024 | Motor Vehicle Account | 2,000,000 | |
| | Nairobi Motors Ltd (Creditor) | | 2,000,000 |
| | *Being purchase of delivery van on credit* | | |

**Ledger Account:**

```
           Motor Vehicle Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Nairobi Motors   2,000,000 | Bal c/d    2,000,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Bal b/d          2,000,000 |
    (Asset remains at historical cost)
```

## 3. DEPRECIATION — METHODS AND ACCOUNTING

### 3.1 Definition

**Depreciation** is the systematic allocation of the depreciable amount of a non-current asset over its estimated useful life (IASB, 2003, para. 6).

- **Depreciable amount** = Cost − Residual (Salvage) Value
- **Useful life** = Period over which the asset is expected to be used
- **Net Book Value (NBV)** = Cost − Accumulated Depreciation (also called Carrying Amount)

### 3.2 Reasons for Depreciation

1. **Wear and tear** — Physical deterioration from use (e.g., a matatu's engine wearing out)
2. **Passage of time** — Age causes deterioration even without use (e.g., a building aging)
3. **Obsolescence** — Technological change makes the asset outdated (e.g., an old computer replaced by newer models)
4. **Depletion** — Extraction of natural resources (e.g., a quarry in Machakos)
5. **Legal/contractual limits** — Expiry of a lease or license

### 3.3 Method 1: Straight-Line Method

The asset is depreciated by an **equal amount each year**.

**Formula:**

```
Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Residual Value) / Useful Life
```

**Worked Example:**

Wanjiku Transport, Nairobi, buys a delivery van:
- Cost: KES 2,000,000
- Estimated residual value: KES 200,000
- Estimated useful life: 5 years

Annual Depreciation = (2,000,000 − 200,000) / 5 = **KES 360,000 per year**

| Year | Cost (KES) | Annual Dep'n (KES) | Accumulated Dep'n (KES) | NBV (KES) |
|------|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|----------|
| 1 | 2,000,000 | 360,000 | 360,000 | 1,640,000 |
| 2 | 2,000,000 | 360,000 | 720,000 | 1,280,000 |
| 3 | 2,000,000 | 360,000 | 1,080,000 | 920,000 |
| 4 | 2,000,000 | 360,000 | 1,440,000 | 560,000 |
| 5 | 2,000,000 | 360,000 | 1,800,000 | 200,000 |

At the end of Year 5, NBV = Residual Value = KES 200,000 ✓

**Journal Entry (each year):**

| Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Depreciation Expense | 360,000 | |
| Provision for Depreciation — Motor Vehicle | | 360,000 |
| *Being annual depreciation charge (straight-line)* | | |

### 3.4 Method 2: Reducing (Diminishing) Balance Method

Depreciation is calculated as a **fixed percentage of the Net Book Value** at the beginning of each year. This gives higher depreciation in early years and lower in later years.

**Formula:**

```
Annual Depreciation = Depreciation Rate × Net Book Value (at start of year)
```

**Worked Example:**

Ochieng Engineering, Kisumu, buys machinery:
- Cost: KES 1,000,000
- Depreciation rate: 25% per annum (reducing balance)

| Year | NBV at Start (KES) | Dep'n (25%) (KES) | Accumulated Dep'n (KES) | NBV at End (KES) |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| 1 | 1,000,000 | 250,000 | 250,000 | 750,000 |
| 2 | 750,000 | 187,500 | 437,500 | 562,500 |
| 3 | 562,500 | 140,625 | 578,125 | 421,875 |
| 4 | 421,875 | 105,469 | 683,594 | 316,406 |
| 5 | 316,406 | 79,102 | 762,696 | 237,304 |

**Note:** Under the reducing balance method, the asset is never fully depreciated to zero.

**Journal Entry (Year 1):**

| Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Depreciation Expense | 250,000 | |
| Provision for Depreciation — Machinery | | 250,000 |
| *Being depreciation at 25% reducing balance, Year 1* | | |

### 3.5 Comparison of Methods

| Feature | Straight-Line | Reducing Balance |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Annual charge | Equal each year | Decreasing each year |
| Early years | Lower charge | Higher charge |
| Later years | Same charge | Lower charge |
| Calculation | Simpler | Slightly more complex |
| Best suited for | Assets that wear evenly (e.g., furniture, buildings) | Assets that lose value quickly early on (e.g., vehicles, computers) |
| IAS 16 compliance | Yes | Yes |

## 4. PROVISION FOR DEPRECIATION ACCOUNT

### 4.1 Concept

The **provision for depreciation account** is a contra-asset account that accumulates the total depreciation charged on a fixed asset since its acquisition. It is maintained separately from the asset account so that the asset continues to be shown at its original historical cost in the ledger, while the accumulated depreciation is deducted on the face of the Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position) to arrive at the Net Book Value (NBV).

**Balance Sheet presentation:**

| | KES | KES |
|--|-----|-----|
| Motor Vehicle (at cost) | | 2,000,000 |
| Less: Provision for Depreciation | | (720,000) |
| **Net Book Value** | | **1,280,000** |

### 4.2 Recording Depreciation — Double Entry

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Depreciation Expense (Income Statement) | ✓ | |
| Provision for Depreciation Account | | ✓ |

The asset account **stays at cost**. The accumulated depreciation is held in the provision account.

### 4.3 Worked Example — Provision for Depreciation Account

Using Wanjiku Transport's van (straight-line, KES 360,000/year):

```
           Motor Vehicle Account (at cost)
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Bank           2,000,000  | Bal c/d      2,000,000
    Bal b/d        2,000,000  |
    (Remains at historical cost throughout)

     Provision for Depreciation — Motor Vehicle
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    YEAR 1:
    Bal c/d          360,000  | Dep'n Expense  360,000
    YEAR 2:
    Bal c/d          720,000  | Bal b/d        360,000
                              | Dep'n Expense  360,000
                     720,000  |                720,000
    YEAR 3:
    Bal c/d        1,080,000  | Bal b/d        720,000
                              | Dep'n Expense  360,000
                   1,080,000  |              1,080,000

        Depreciation Expense Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Prov for Dep'n   360,000  | P&L            360,000
    (Each year — transferred to the Income Statement)
```

### 4.4 Effect on Financial Statements

- **Income Statement (P&L):** Depreciation expense reduces profit each year.
- **Balance Sheet:** The provision for depreciation is deducted from the asset's cost to show the NBV. The provision account balance grows each year as depreciation accumulates.

## 5. DISPOSAL OF FIXED ASSETS

### 5.1 When Are Assets Disposed Of?

Assets are disposed of when they are:
- Sold (e.g., selling an old matatu)
- Traded in for a new asset
- Scrapped (e.g., a broken-down machine)
- Stolen or destroyed

### 5.2 Accounting for Disposal — Steps and Journal Entries

A **Disposal Account** is opened to calculate the profit or loss on disposal.

| Step | Narrative | Debit | Credit |
|------|-----------|-------|--------|
| 1 | Remove asset at cost | Disposal Account | Asset Account (at cost) |
| 2 | Remove accumulated depreciation | Provision for Depreciation Account | Disposal Account |
| 3 | Record proceeds received | Cash/Bank | Disposal Account |
| 4a | If profit (credit balance in Disposal A/c) | Disposal Account | Profit & Loss (income) |
| 4b | If loss (debit balance in Disposal A/c) | Profit & Loss (expense) | Disposal Account |

### 5.3 Worked Example: Disposal at a Profit

**Kamau Traders, Nakuru:**

Kamau bought office furniture on 1 January 2023 for KES 200,000. Depreciation: 10% straight-line. On 1 January 2026 (after 3 years), the furniture is sold for KES 160,000 cash.

**Calculations:**
- Cost: KES 200,000
- Accumulated depreciation (3 years × 10% × 200,000): KES 60,000
- NBV at disposal: KES 140,000
- Sale proceeds: KES 160,000
- **Profit on disposal: KES 20,000**

**Journal Entries:**

| Step | Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Disposal Account | 200,000 | |
| | Furniture Account (at cost) | | 200,000 |
| 2 | Provision for Depreciation — Furniture | 60,000 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 60,000 |
| 3 | Cash/Bank | 160,000 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 160,000 |
| 4 | Disposal Account | 20,000 | |
| | Profit & Loss (Profit on Disposal) | | 20,000 |

**Disposal Account:**

```
              Furniture Disposal Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Furniture (cost)  200,000 | Prov for Dep'n  60,000
    P&L (profit)       20,000 | Cash           160,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                      220,000 |                220,000
```

**Effect on Income Statement:** Profit on disposal of KES 20,000 is recorded as **income**.

### 5.4 Worked Example: Disposal at a Loss

**Atieno Supplies, Kisumu:**

Atieno bought a computer on 1 January 2024 for KES 120,000. Depreciation: 25% reducing balance. On 31 December 2025 (after 2 years), the computer is sold for KES 50,000.

**Calculations:**
- Cost: KES 120,000
- Year 1 depreciation: 25% × 120,000 = KES 30,000. NBV = 90,000
- Year 2 depreciation: 25% × 90,000 = KES 22,500. NBV = 67,500
- Accumulated depreciation: KES 52,500
- NBV at disposal: KES 67,500
- Sale proceeds: KES 50,000
- **Loss on disposal: KES 17,500**

**Journal Entries:**

| Step | Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Disposal Account | 120,000 | |
| | Computer Account (at cost) | | 120,000 |
| 2 | Provision for Depreciation — Computer | 52,500 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 52,500 |
| 3 | Cash/Bank | 50,000 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 50,000 |
| 4 | Profit & Loss (Loss on Disposal) | 17,500 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 17,500 |

**Disposal Account:**

```
              Computer Disposal Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Computer (cost)   120,000 | Prov for Dep'n  52,500
                              | Cash            50,000
                              | P&L (loss)      17,500
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                      120,000 |                120,000
```

**Effect on Income Statement:** Loss on disposal of KES 17,500 is recorded as an **expense**.

## 6. ACCOUNTING PRINCIPLES IN HANDLING FIXED ASSETS

The following accounting principles and standards govern the treatment of fixed assets:

| Principle / Standard | Application to Fixed Assets |
|---------------------|----------------------------|
| **Historical Cost Principle** | Fixed assets are initially recorded at cost — the amount paid to acquire and bring the asset into use (IAS 16, para. 15) |
| **Matching Principle (Accruals)** | Depreciation is charged in the period the asset generates revenue, matching the cost of the asset to the income it helps produce |
| **Consistency Principle** | The same depreciation method must be applied consistently from period to period; changes require disclosure (IAS 16, para. 61) |
| **Prudence** | Assets should not be overstated; if NBV exceeds recoverable amount, an impairment loss is recognised (IAS 36) |
| **Going Concern** | Fixed assets are depreciated over their useful life on the assumption the business will continue to operate |
| **Materiality** | Low-value items (e.g., a stapler) may be expensed immediately rather than capitalised, even if they meet the definition of a fixed asset |
| **Substance over Form** | Assets held under finance leases are capitalised even though legal ownership may not have passed (IFRS 16) |

**Summary of IAS 16 requirements:**
- Initial recognition: at cost
- Subsequent measurement: cost model (cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment) or revaluation model
- Depreciation method: must reflect the pattern of consumption of economic benefits
- Residual value and useful life: reviewed at least at each financial year-end

### Question 1: Depreciation Calculation

### Question 2: Disposal of a Fixed Asset

Mutua Logistics, Machakos, purchased a lorry on 1 January 2023 for KES 4,000,000. Depreciation is charged at 20% per annum using the straight-line method with no residual value. On 30 June 2025, the lorry was sold for KES 2,200,000.

**Required:**
(a) Calculate the accumulated depreciation at the date of disposal
(b) Prepare the Disposal Account and all journal entries
(c) State whether there was a profit or loss on disposal

**Solution:**

**(a) Accumulated Depreciation:**

Annual depreciation = 20% × 4,000,000 = KES 800,000

- 2023: KES 800,000 (full year)
- 2024: KES 800,000 (full year)
- 2025: KES 400,000 (half year — Jan to June)
- **Total accumulated depreciation = KES 2,000,000**

NBV at disposal = 4,000,000 − 2,000,000 = KES 2,000,000

**(b) Journal Entries and Disposal Account:**

| Step | Account | Dr (KES) | Cr (KES) |
|------|---------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Disposal Account | 4,000,000 | |
| | Lorry Account (at cost) | | 4,000,000 |
| 2 | Provision for Depreciation — Lorry | 2,000,000 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 2,000,000 |
| 3 | Cash/Bank | 2,200,000 | |
| | Disposal Account | | 2,200,000 |
| 4 | Disposal Account | 200,000 | |
| | Profit & Loss (Profit on Disposal) | | 200,000 |

```
              Lorry Disposal Account
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Dr                          Cr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Lorry (cost)    4,000,000 | Prov for Dep'n 2,000,000
    P&L (profit)      200,000 | Cash           2,200,000
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
                    4,200,000 |                4,200,000
```

**(c)** There is a **profit on disposal of KES 200,000**, which will appear as income in the Statement of Profit or Loss.

### Question 3: Discussion

(a) Explain why land is generally not depreciated but buildings are.

**Model Answer:**
Land has an unlimited useful life — it does not wear out, become obsolete, or get consumed. Therefore, there is no cost to allocate over a finite period. Buildings, however, have a finite useful life due to wear and tear, weather damage, and eventual need for replacement. Hence buildings are depreciated to allocate their cost over their useful life in accordance with IAS 16.

(b) A business in Nairobi bought computers in 2020. Explain why the reducing balance method may be more appropriate than the straight-line method for depreciating these computers.

**Model Answer:**
Computers lose value rapidly in their early years due to technological obsolescence — newer, faster models are released frequently. The reducing balance method charges higher depreciation in early years and lower in later years, which better reflects the actual pattern of economic benefit consumption. It also better matches the higher maintenance costs in later years with lower depreciation charges, giving a more even total cost of ownership across the asset's life. This is consistent with the IAS 16 requirement that the depreciation method should reflect the pattern in which the asset's future economic benefits are expected to be consumed.

(c) State **two** accounting principles that support the practice of depreciating fixed assets.

**Model Answer:**
1. **Matching (Accruals) Principle** — Depreciation ensures that the cost of a fixed asset is matched against the revenue it helps generate in each accounting period, rather than being charged entirely in the year of purchase.
2. **Prudence** — Assets should not be overstated on the Balance Sheet. Charging depreciation reduces the carrying amount of the asset to reflect its consumption and prevents overstatement of profit.
